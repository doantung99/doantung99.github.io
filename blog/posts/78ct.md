## Summary

78CT (the source calls it "Rawr Dragon Lake") is a three-stage OSINT challenge. An aerial photo of a dragon-shaped park geolocates to Hồ điều hòa Hồ Sơn (Công viên Hồ Sơn) in Tuy Hòa, Phú Yên, Vietnam; on that location's Google Maps page there is a planted **review containing the hashtag `#v1tnamese`**; and the lake-themed word salad in that review hides a Base64 string through abnormal capitalization and digits, which decodes to the flag.

The honest framing of how I solved it: an LLM did almost all of the grinding — proposing Vietnamese search terms, reasoning about the dragon-and-pearl motif, spotting the steganographic pattern, and writing the extractor. My job was recognition and steering. I clocked it as "geolocation → on-platform pivot → text stego," pointed the model at each stage, killed two of its wrong guesses, and verified the decode at the end. The model ground; I drove.

## Solution

### Stage 0 — read the puzzle shape before touching tools

The prompt hands you an aerial image of a park whose lake is shaped like a dragon curling next to a round "pearl" island, plus flavor text about a lake "in the heart of my hometown" and something "left behind" at the lake. Two halves jump out immediately:

- **A geolocation half.** A distinctive man-made shape from above is the easiest kind of geolocation target, because the shape itself is a near-unique fingerprint you can describe in words and search for.
- **A "decode what was left behind" half.** "Left behind at the lake" is challenge-author language for *a planted artifact on the location's public page* — almost always a Google Maps review, a photo caption, or a Street View detail. The word "decode" tells you the artifact is encoded, not just hidden.

That reading is the single most important move in the whole solve, and it is a *human* move. The shape of the challenge ("identify a place, then pivot to user-generated content on that place's map listing, then decode") is something you recognize from having seen the genre. I wrote it down before prompting so the model had a skeleton to fill instead of wandering.

### Stage 1 — geolocate the dragon

The fingerprint here is not just "a lake" but the **dragon-nhả-ngọc** ("dragon spitting a pearl") motif: a sinuous water body beside a circular feature. That is a recognizable Vietnamese landscape-design trope, and the flavor text ("hometown," Vietnamese framing) strongly implies a Vietnamese location, so the right search language is Vietnamese, not English. The model generated and I refined these queries:

```text
công viên hình rồng hồ Việt Nam
công viên rồng nhả ngọc Phú Yên
hồ điều hòa hình rồng Tuy Hòa
```

The second and third queries converge on the same place: **Hồ điều hòa Hồ Sơn / Công viên Hồ Sơn**, in Phường 5, TP Tuy Hòa, Phú Yên, Vietnam. Cross-checking the aerial layout against the satellite view of that park confirms the dragon body and the pearl island line up with the challenge image.

```text
Target location: Hồ điều hòa Hồ Sơn, Tuy Hòa, Phú Yên, Vietnam
```

**Why Vietnamese search terms matter (the key insight of this stage):** an English query like "dragon-shaped lake Vietnam" surfaces tourist listicles and the famous Đà Nẵng Dragon Bridge — neither is this park. The "spitting a pearl" descriptor (`nhả ngọc`) plus the province guess (`Phú Yên`) is what collapses the search space. Searching in the local language is the standard unlock for in-country geolocation, and it was the first correction I made to the model's instinct to search in English.

**Dead-end that actually cost time:** the model's first association for "dragon" + "Vietnam" was the Đà Nẵng Dragon Bridge. That is a *bridge*, not a lake-and-pearl park, and the aerial silhouette doesn't match. I had to explicitly tell it to ignore famous dragon landmarks and treat the satellite *silhouette* — a curved water body next to a round island — as the only thing that has to match. That re-anchoring on the shape, not the word "dragon," is what got it to Hồ Sơn.

### Stage 2 — the on-platform pivot to the planted review (the step that's easy to miss)

Geolocating the park is *not* the flag. The "left behind" artifact lives on the **Google Maps page for Hồ điều hòa Hồ Sơn**. Open that listing and read the user reviews; one of them is the plant. You identify it instantly because it ends with the CTF-y hashtag:

```text
#v1tnamese
```

That hashtag is the author's signature — `v1t` is the flag prefix, so `#v1tnamese` is a deliberate breadcrumb confirming "this review is the challenge, keep going." The review body is a run of lake/scenery vocabulary that reads almost-but-not-quite like a real review:

```text
traVelers MAjESTIC reFlections sUrface LAKeSIDE sh1mering ScENERY AMAzING
eXpanse lak3front glImmering BREEzES suNlit harGor ExPLORERS DREAMsCAPE
LAKeVIEW riVerside 9 CALMsNESS harMor higHway hazY AMAzINGLY eXplore
MAzEWORK BROAdWAY HARBoR sunMist tranTquil loVely OFfERS ARcHITECTS
gleaminG MEADOwS harb0r skYline HAzE suNset 9
#v1tnamese
```

This is the stage the short version of the writeup glossed over, and it's the one a solver is most likely to fumble: you can have the right park and still stall because you never opened the reviews. **The pivot rule for this genre: once you've geolocated, immediately read the target's reviews, photos, and Q&A on Google Maps for a planted, CTF-flavored string.** The `#v1tnamese` tag is exactly the kind of marker that tells you you've found it.

### Stage 3 — read the steganography

The review words are real English words drawn from a "peaceful lakeside" theme, which is camouflage. The signal is in the *one character per word that doesn't belong*:

```text
traVelers    -> V   (one stray uppercase in a lowercase word)
MAjESTIC     -> j   (one stray lowercase in an uppercase word)
reFlections  -> F
sUrface      -> U
LAKeSIDE     -> e
sh1mering    -> 1   (a digit where a letter should be)
lak3front    -> 3
harb0r       -> 0
```

So the extraction rule is a single, consistent idea — *take the character that is anomalous for its word*:

- a mostly-lowercase word with exactly one uppercase letter → take the uppercase letter;
- a mostly-uppercase word with exactly one lowercase letter → take the lowercase letter;
- a word containing a digit → take the digit.

**Two gotchas that matter for a correct extraction:**

1. **The lone `9` tokens are real payload, not noise.** The string contains two standalone `9` tokens (one mid-stream, one at the end). They are digits, so they get emitted as-is. If you write your extractor to only look at letters, or to skip single-character tokens, you drop two characters and your Base64 length becomes invalid (Base64 must be a multiple of 4). Those two `9`s are part of the encoded tail; dropping them silently corrupts the end of the flag.
2. **Order is left-to-right, token by token, no sorting.** The output is a positional stream; the only correct ordering is the order the words appear. (Obvious in hindsight, but it's a place where an over-clever model will try to "clean up" the data.)

Concatenating the anomalous character of every token, in order, yields:

```text
VjFUe1czX3IzNGxseV9sMHYzXzdoMTVfcGw0YzN9
```

This is recognizably Base64: only Base64-alphabet characters, length 40 (a multiple of 4), and — the tell that confirms the whole approach — the prefix `VjFU` is what `V1T` Base64-encodes to. Seeing the flag prefix appear in the *encoded* string is the moment you know the extraction rule was exactly right.

### Stage 4 — decode

```bash
echo 'VjFUe1czX3IzNGxseV9sMHYzXzdoMTVfcGw0YzN9' | base64 -d
# -> V1T{W3_r34lly_l0v3_7h15_pl4c3}
```

The decoded prefix is uppercase `V1T{...}`; the challenge format is `v1t{...}`, so normalize the case of the prefix. The flag is `v1t{W3_r34lly_l0v3_7h15_pl4c3}` — fittingly, "we really love this place," a nod to the hometown lake.

### End-to-end script (review text → printed flag)

This is the one complete path. Paste the `#v1tnamese` review body in and it prints the Base64 and the flag.

```python
#!/usr/bin/env python3
"""78CT / Rawr Dragon Lake — decode the #v1tnamese Google Maps review.

Pipeline:
  1. (manual OSINT) aerial dragon park  -> Hồ điều hòa Hồ Sơn, Tuy Hòa, Phú Yên
  2. (manual OSINT) open its Google Maps page, find the review ending in #v1tnamese
  3. (this script)  per-word anomaly extraction -> Base64 -> flag
"""
import base64
import re

# The review body as it appears on the Google Maps listing (drop the #v1tnamese line).
review = """
traVelers MAjESTIC reFlections sUrface LAKeSIDE sh1mering ScENERY AMAzING
eXpanse lak3front glImmering BREEzES suNlit harGor ExPLORERS DREAMsCAPE
LAKeVIEW riVerside 9 CALMsNESS harMor higHway hazY AMAzINGLY eXplore
MAzEWORK BROAdWAY HARBoR sunMist tranTquil loVely OFfERS ARcHITECTS
gleaminG MEADOwS harb0r skYline HAzE suNset 9
"""

def anomalous_char(word: str):
    """Return the single character that doesn't fit the word's casing, or a digit."""
    digits = [c for c in word if c.isdigit()]
    lowers = [c for c in word if c.islower()]
    uppers = [c for c in word if c.isupper()]
    if digits:                                   # digit beats casing (incl. lone '9')
        return digits[0]
    if len(uppers) == 1 and len(lowers) > 1:     # one stray uppercase in a lowercase word
        return uppers[0]
    if len(lowers) == 1 and len(uppers) > 1:     # one stray lowercase in an uppercase word
        return lowers[0]
    return None                                  # uniform word -> contributes nothing

chars = []
for token in re.findall(r"[A-Za-z0-9]+", review):   # left-to-right, preserve order
    c = anomalous_char(token)
    if c is not None:
        chars.append(c)

b64 = "".join(chars)
print("base64:", b64)

decoded = base64.b64decode(b64).decode()
flag = "v1t{" + decoded.split("{", 1)[1] if "{" in decoded else decoded
print("flag:  ", flag)
```

Output:

```text
base64: VjFUe1czX3IzNGxseV9sMHYzXzdoMTVfcGw0YzN9
flag:   v1t{W3_r34lly_l0v3_7h15_pl4c3}
```

## Lessons learned - prompting the AI

**Whenever you face a multi-stage "geolocate-then-pivot-to-user-content" OSINT challenge** — an image (or coordinates, or a place name) that you have to identify, after which the actual flag is a *planted artifact on that place's public page* (Google Maps reviews/photos/Q&A, a Wikipedia talk page, a TripAdvisor entry, a Wikimedia caption, a geocache log) that is then encoded/stego'd — the prompts below transfer almost verbatim. This class has a fixed three-beat structure (identify location → pivot to UGC on that location → decode the plant), and an LLM is fast at beats 1 and 3 but blind to beat 2. The reusable lesson is: **make the model treat it as a known 3-stage pipeline up front, search in the venue's native language, anchor identification on shape/uniqueness rather than fame, and hand it the encoding scheme rather than asking "what is this?"** This instance was a Vietnamese dragon-lake park, a `#v1tnamese`-tagged Maps review, and case/digit-anomaly Base64 — but swap those nouns and the recipe holds.

**Reusable prompts for this class** (copy-paste, swap the bracketed nouns):

1. Force the full pipeline before the model sees the artifact, so it can't declare victory at identification:

> "This is a multi-stage OSINT challenge with THREE stages: (1) identify the location in this [image/clue], (2) open that location's [Google Maps / TripAdvisor / Wikipedia] page and find a planted user-generated artifact — it will carry a CTF-style marker such as a hashtag, an out-of-place review, or a caption, (3) decode the text in that artifact. Do NOT stop at stage 1 — the place name is not the flag. After you name the location, tell me exactly which UGC sections (reviews, photos, Q&A) to open."

2. Identification in the right language, anchored on the unique feature and steered off the famous decoy:

> "Search in [the venue's native language], not English. The distinctive feature is [describe the shape/silhouette/sign in plain words]. IGNORE famous landmarks that merely share the keyword '[keyword]' — match the satellite SILHOUETTE / the literal visible detail, not the word. Give me 3 native-language queries, each narrowed by a [province/region/city] guess."

3. Pivot enumeration — make the model list where plants hide, so it doesn't stall after identifying:

> "We have the location. List every place on its public page where a challenge author could plant a string: each review (sort newest and 'most relevant'), every uploaded photo's caption, the Q&A, and the 'updates by owner' section. Flag anything containing a CTF marker (hashtag, flag-prefix substring, or text that reads like camouflage rather than a real review)."

4. Decode — name the scheme you already half-see instead of asking open-endedly (this is what avoids cipher rabbit holes):

> "Each [lake-themed] word has exactly one anomalous character — a stray uppercase in a lowercase word, a stray lowercase in an uppercase word, or a digit where a letter belongs. Extract that one character per token IN ORDER, INCLUDING standalone digit tokens, concatenate, and Base64-decode. Write the extractor and print BOTH the raw concatenation and the decode."

**What to tell the model to focus on, and the classic dead-ends to forbid up front:**
- *Focus:* native-language search; matching the unique *silhouette / literal detail* rather than a keyword; explicit enumeration of UGC surfaces (reviews/photos/Q&A/owner posts) after identification; per-character/positional extraction that preserves token order; testing Base64 (alphabet + length-multiple-of-4 + recognizable encoded prefix) before any classical cipher.
- *Avoid (state these as bans in your prompt):* English-only geolocation searches; pattern-matching to the famous landmark that shares the keyword (here, Đà Nẵng Dragon Bridge — the model's first guess every time); declaring the solve done at the location name; treating standalone digits or single-character tokens as noise; reordering/sorting the extracted stream; jumping to ROT13/Vigenère/XOR before ruling Base64 in or out.

**How to verify the model's output for this class (so you catch hallucinations):**
- *Identification:* refuse the answer until the model has matched the actual silhouette/feature against the satellite or Street View of the *named* place — not a name that merely "sounds like" the keyword. A confident place name with no shape cross-check is a hallucination tell.
- *Pivot:* make it quote the exact planted string and say which UGC surface it came from; if it can't produce a verbatim marker (hashtag / flag-prefix substring), it hasn't actually found the plant.
- *Extraction/decode:* two cheap invariants catch nearly every extraction bug — (a) the candidate string is valid for its claimed encoding (for Base64, length divisible by 4 and only `A–Za–z0–9+/=`); (b) the *encoded* string begins with `Base64(flag_prefix)` — here `VjFU` == `Base64("V1T")`. If the decode would be off by even one extracted character, that prefix won't appear, so you know the chain is right *before* running `base64 -d`. The model's first extractor here dropped the lone `9` tokens, giving a non-multiple-of-4 length and a mangled tail — the length check alone exposed it.

**Fast-path prompt recipe for the class:** *"Tell the model it's a 3-stage OSINT solve (native-language silhouette/detail identification → enumerate-and-pivot to the location's UGC page for a marker-tagged plant → decode), ban English-only search and famous-landmark guesses, describe the casing/digit/positional anomaly yourself and make it write an encoding-first extractor — then verify by confirming the encoded prefix equals `Base64(flag_prefix)` and the candidate length matches the encoding's rules before decoding."*
